\section{Sparse Autoencoder}

\input{figures/latent_space/latent_space_sparse_autoencoders.tex}

As discussed previously, interpretability approaches based on latent directions face inherent limitations.
Empirically, only a restricted subset of concepte appears to admit a linear representation, and even these directions are often affected by significant entanglement with other concepte.

To account for this behavior, a complementary hypothesis has been introduced, extending the assumption of linear concepte encoding in neural networks.
It posits that semantic concepte are preferentially represented in a linear fashion, with each concept ideally corresponding to a distinct direction in latent space.
In this idealized setting, semantic attributes would form an approximately orthogonal basis, where each direction independently controls a single factor of variation.

We first consider an idealized representation of the latent space in which semantic concepts are perfectly disentangled.
Let \( \mathbf{h} \in \mathbb{R}^d \) denote a latent embedding.
In this setting, each concept is associated with a unique direction \( \mathbf{v}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d \), and the representation of a given input can be expressed as a linear combination of these concept directions:
\[
\mathbf{h} = \sum_{i=1}^{m} a_i \mathbf{v}_i,
\]
where \( a_i \in \mathbb{R} \) represents the activation of concept \( i \).

In the ideal case, the set of concept directions is assumed to be approximately orthogonal, such that each direction independently controls a single semantic factor.
Moreover, for a given input, only a small subset of coefficients \( a_i \) is non-zero, so that each embedding corresponds to a sparse combination of a few active concepts.

In this idealized setting, concept directions are assumed to form a structured basis of the latent space, in which each concept is independently controllable.

When the representational capacity of the model is limited, such a decomposition leads to interference between concepte.
In this regime, multiple semantic concepte are encoded within shared directions, a phenomenon known as \emph{superposition}.
This can be interpreted as a consequence of compression pressure, where the model prioritizes efficient encoding over strict factorization of concepte.

This perspective is closely related to recent work in mechanistic interpretability, where neural representations are assumed to decompose into sparse, approximately linear concepte.
It also connects naturally to classical results in dictionary learning and sparse coding, where signals are represented as sparse combinations of elements from an overcomplete basis.

Sparse autoencoders instantiate this idea in practice by learning an overcomplete dictionary of concepte while enforcing sparsity constraints on activations.
From this perspective, they can be interpreted as a tool for recovering a more disentangled basis of concepte from a superposed representation space.

Despite their empirical success, sparse autoencoders rely on strong structural assumptions that are not always justified.
First, the learned concepte are not guaranteed to be identifiable: different training runs may yield different decompositions of the same latent space, even when the reconstruction performance is similar.
Second, the assumption that the underlying representation is approximately linearly decomposable may not hold uniformly across all regions of the latent space, especially in highly nonlinear regimes.
Finally, sparsity itself introduces a modeling bias that may favor certain types of concepte while suppressing others.

These limitations suggest that sparse autoencoders, while powerful, do not fully resolve the problem of entanglement in neural representations.
They instead provide one possible coordinate system in which parts of the structure become more explicit, but not necessarily a complete or canonical one.

From this perspective, both interpretable directions and sparse autoencoders rely on a shared underlying assumption: that semantic structure can be recovered through linear or approximately linear decompositions.
While this assumption has led to significant empirical progress, it remains theoretically fragile.

The following section therefore introduces a complementary approach that relaxes this constraint and does not require a globally linear feature decomposition of the latent space.
