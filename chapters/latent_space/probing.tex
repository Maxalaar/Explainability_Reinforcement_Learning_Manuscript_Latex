\section{Probing}

The earliest methods for investigating latent spaces relied on the use of probes in order to study the presence or absence of interpretable concepts within the internal representations of deep neural networks.

The general idea behind probing is to determine whether a human interpretable concept is encoded within the latent space learned by the model.
More specifically, a concept is considered, and the objective is to evaluate whether this concept is represented in the internal representations of the network.
The underlying hypothesis is that, if the concept is useful for solving the learned task, then the network should encode information allowing it to be easily distinguished.

A probing analysis begins by identifying a set of interpretable concepts that the network could potentially use to accomplish its task.
Each example in the dataset is then annotated to indicate the presence or absence of the studied concept.

The data are subsequently projected into the latent space of the previously trained model.
From these latent representations, a classifier is trained in a supervised manner to predict the presence of the considered concept.
The role of this probe is therefore to evaluate whether the information associated with the concept is accessible from the internal representations of the network.

The classifier used to detect the presence or absence of a concept must remain intentionally simple.
Indeed, if the probe has too much learning capacity, it may reconstruct the target information from complex correlations that do not necessarily correspond to a representation explicitly encoded in the latent space.
In such a case, it becomes difficult to determine whether the concept is genuinely represented by the network or merely reconstructed through the expressive power of the classifier.

For this reason, probing methods frequently rely on linear classifiers, such as logistic regression or linear separation methods.
The objective is to determine whether the concept can be extracted in a simple manner from the latent representations.
A linear separation indeed suggests that the information is easily accessible to the neural network and potentially exploitable during decision making.

If the classifier achieves performance significantly above chance level, this suggests that the latent space contains exploitable information related to the considered concept.
In other words, the neural network encodes features correlated with this notion, indicating that it may rely on this information during its decision-making process.

Probing techniques therefore constitute an important first approach for studying the interpretability of deep neural networks \cite{li2024emergentworldrepresentationsexploring, karvonen2024emergentworldmodelslatent, McGrath_2022}.
In particular, they make it possible to evaluate to what extent internal representations correspond to concepts understandable by humans.

However, these methods present several important limitations.
First, they can only analyze concepts defined in advance by the experimenter.
As a result, they are unable to reveal emergent representations or unexpected internal strategies learned by the model.
Moreover, they generally require costly human annotations, which limits their scalability and may introduce biases related to the annotators’ choices.

Another important limitation lies in the interpretation of the results.
The fact that a concept can be decoded from latent representations does not necessarily imply that the model actually uses this information to perform its task.
Probing therefore mainly measures the accessibility of information within the latent space, without always establishing its causal role in the model’s behavior.

Despite these limitations, probing approaches remain a central tool for analyzing latent representations.
Furthermore, some methods extend this approach by performing interventions directly within the latent space in order to study the causal impact of specific dimensions or concepts on the model’s behavior.
