\section{Conclusion}

The techniques presented in this chapter: clustering, linear probing, interpretable directions, and sparse autoencoders, do not individually constitute proof of the existence of concepts in the latent space.
None of them allows us to assert with certainty that the internal representations of a model encode discrete and well-defined semantic entities.
What these approaches offer, however, is a converging body of evidence: the geometric structure of the latent space is coherent, stable, and amenable to semantically meaningful interpretation across very different methods.
In the absence of a credible alternative hypothesis to explain both the generalization capacity of deep learning and the richness of the representations it produces, the conceptual hypothesis remains the most parsimonious and best supported.

However, the vast majority of these approaches rest, explicitly or implicitly, on a linearity assumption: semantic relationships are assumed to translate into linear operations in the latent space.
Deep clustering stands as a notable exception, seeking structures that are not necessarily linear.
It is precisely this linearity assumption that will be examined in the remainder of this work: our explainability method will build upon it as a geometric foundation, exploring the extent to which a model's decisions can be explained through linear decompositions of its latent space.
